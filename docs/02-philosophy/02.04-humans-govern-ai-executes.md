# Humans Govern, AI Executes

Version: 1.0
Status: Canonical
Category: Foundation

---

# 1. Purpose

This document establishes the fourth philosophical principle of cmdOS:

> Humans define authority.
>
> AI performs delegated execution.

cmdOS is designed to augment human capability, not replace human judgment.

Every execution performed by AI ultimately derives its legitimacy from human authority.

---

# 2. Governance Before Execution

Execution without governance is unacceptable.

Before any action is performed, cmdOS shall determine:

- who requested the action
- whether the requester has authority
- which policies apply
- what limitations exist
- whether additional approval is required

Governance always precedes execution.

---

# 3. Human Authority

Authority originates only from legitimate principals.

Examples include:

- individual users
- organizations
- delegated administrators
- automated systems explicitly authorized by humans

AI never creates authority.

AI only consumes delegated authority.

---

# 4. AI as an Executor

The primary responsibility of AI is execution.

AI may:

- interpret intent
- generate plans
- optimize workflows
- coordinate agents
- monitor execution
- recover from failures
- explain decisions

AI shall not independently redefine objectives or expand its authority.

---

# 5. Separation of Responsibilities

Human responsibilities include:

- defining objectives
- granting permissions
- approving sensitive actions
- establishing organizational policies
- accepting accountability

AI responsibilities include:

- understanding intent
- planning execution
- coordinating capabilities
- monitoring progress
- reporting outcomes

This separation preserves accountability.

---

# 6. Delegated Authority

Authority may be delegated.

Delegation shall specify:

- scope
- duration
- permitted actions
- execution limits
- revocation conditions

Delegation never transfers ownership of authority.

Ownership always remains with the delegating principal.

---

# 7. Approval Boundaries

Certain actions require explicit approval regardless of AI confidence.

Examples include:

- financial transactions
- legal commitments
- identity changes
- credential management
- security policy modification
- destructive operations
- irreversible actions

Approval requirements are determined by policy rather than model confidence.

---

# 8. Human Override

Every execution shall remain interruptible.

Authorized humans may:

- pause execution
- modify execution
- cancel execution
- revoke permissions
- replace execution plans

No AI execution shall become irrevocable solely through autonomous decision-making.

---

# 9. AI Recommendations

AI may recommend actions.

Recommendations are distinct from decisions.

Example:

AI Recommendation:

> Upgrade server capacity.

Human Decision:

> Approved.

Execution:

> Perform infrastructure upgrade.

The recommendation process never bypasses governance.

---

# 10. Organizational Governance

Organizations may define governance structures.

Examples include:

- role-based authority
- approval chains
- separation of duties
- compliance workflows
- departmental ownership
- delegated administration

cmdOS shall enforce these governance structures consistently across all execution pathways.

---

# 11. Accountability

Every execution must have an accountable owner.

Possible owners include:

- individual users
- organizational administrators
- approved automation
- authorized service accounts

Anonymous authority is prohibited.

---

# 12. AI Limitations

AI shall never:

- invent permissions
- fabricate approvals
- bypass policies
- impersonate users
- escalate privileges
- remove audit records
- conceal execution

These limitations are architectural requirements.

---

# 13. Governance Lifecycle

Every governed execution follows the same lifecycle.

```
Intent

↓

Identity Verification

↓

Authority Resolution

↓

Policy Evaluation

↓

Approval (if required)

↓

Execution

↓

Audit

↓

Completion
```

No execution bypasses governance.

---

# 14. Design Principles

Governed execution follows these principles.

### Authority Is Explicit

Authority must be granted, never assumed.

---

### Governance Is Continuous

Governance applies before, during, and after execution.

---

### Accountability Is Traceable

Every execution is linked to an accountable principal.

---

### Delegation Is Limited

Delegated authority is always constrained.

---

### Override Is Always Available

Authorized humans retain ultimate control.

---

# 15. Architectural Consequences

This philosophy requires dedicated governance components.

Including:

- Identity Framework
- Authority Resolution Engine
- Permission System
- Policy Engine
- Approval Engine
- Audit Framework
- Organization Model
- Delegation Framework

Together, these components ensure that execution always remains under human governance.

---

# 16. Governance Invariants

The following statements shall always remain true.

1. Humans define authority.
2. AI executes delegated work.
3. Authority precedes execution.
4. Governance precedes automation.
5. Every execution has an accountable owner.
6. Authority is never inferred.
7. Human override is always available.
8. AI never expands its own authority.

These invariants apply across the entire cmdOS platform.

---

# 17. Relationship to the Specification

This document establishes the governance philosophy of cmdOS.

Future specifications—including Identity Model, Organization Model, Permission Framework, Policy Engine, Approval Engine, Delegation Framework, and Kernel—shall implement these principles.

Governance remains independent of intelligence providers and execution technologies.

---

# 18. Summary

Human intent gives purpose.

Human authority grants legitimacy.

AI provides execution.

cmdOS is therefore built on a clear separation of responsibilities:

Humans govern.

AI executes.

This principle ensures that intelligence remains aligned with human objectives, organizational policies, and accountable decision-making throughout the lifecycle of every execution.
