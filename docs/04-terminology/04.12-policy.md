# Policy

Version: 1.0
Status: Canonical
Category: Foundation

---

# 1. Purpose

This document defines the canonical meaning of **Policy** within cmdOS.

Policy is the governance mechanism that determines what is permitted, prohibited, required, or restricted throughout the AI Execution Operating System.

Every execution within cmdOS operates under one or more Policies.

Policies ensure that intelligence never exceeds authority.

---

# 2. Definition

A **Policy** is a formally defined, enforceable rule that governs the behavior of platform entities.

Policies evaluate actions before, during, and after execution.

A Policy may:

- allow
- deny
- require approval
- restrict
- limit
- modify execution behavior

Policies are authoritative.

---

# 3. Policy Is Not Permission

Permissions describe available authority.

Policies determine whether that authority may be exercised.

Example:

```
Permission

↓

Can Execute

↓

Policy

↓

Execution Allowed?
```

Permissions grant capability.

Policies govern capability.

---

# 4. Policy Is Not Security

Security provides protective mechanisms.

Policy defines governing rules.

```
Authentication

↓

Authorization

↓

Policy Evaluation

↓

Execution
```

Security enforces.

Policy decides.

---

# 5. Policy Characteristics

Every Policy shall be:

- explicit
- deterministic
- enforceable
- observable
- versioned
- auditable
- implementation independent

Hidden Policies are prohibited.

---

# 6. Policy Identity

Every Policy shall possess a globally unique identifier.

Minimum metadata includes:

- Policy ID
- Name
- Version
- Owner
- Scope
- Priority
- Status
- Effective Date
- Expiration Date

Identity enables governance and auditing.

---

# 7. Policy Scope

A Policy may govern:

- Users
- Agents
- Sessions
- Executions
- Workflows
- Capabilities
- Resources
- Organizations
- APIs
- Infrastructure

Scope shall be explicitly defined.

---

# 8. Policy Lifecycle

Every Policy progresses through a managed lifecycle.

```
Draft

↓

Validated

↓

Approved

↓

Active

↓

Deprecated

↓

Retired
```

Lifecycle transitions shall remain observable.

---

# 9. Policy Evaluation

Policy evaluation occurs whenever governed actions are attempted.

Evaluation considers:

- identity
- permissions
- context
- execution state
- resource availability
- organizational rules
- trust level

Evaluation produces a deterministic decision.

---

# 10. Policy Decisions

Policy evaluation shall produce one of the following outcomes.

- Allow
- Deny
- Require Approval
- Retry Later
- Escalate
- Restrict

No undefined outcome is permitted.

---

# 11. Policy Hierarchy

Policies may exist at multiple levels.

```
Platform Policy

↓

Organization Policy

↓

Project Policy

↓

Workflow Policy

↓

Execution Policy
```

Higher-level Policies take precedence unless explicitly overridden by platform governance.

---

# 12. Policy Composition

Multiple Policies may be evaluated together.

Evaluation strategies include:

- priority order
- logical conjunction
- logical disjunction
- consensus
- weighted evaluation

Composition rules shall be deterministic.

---

# 13. Policy Conflicts

Conflicting Policies shall be resolved explicitly.

Conflict resolution may consider:

- priority
- specificity
- scope
- version
- organizational authority

Undefined conflict behavior is prohibited.

---

# 14. Policy Enforcement

Approved Policies shall be enforced continuously.

Enforcement may occur:

- before execution
- during execution
- after execution
- during recovery
- during resource allocation

Policy enforcement is continuous.

---

# 15. Policy Observability

Every Policy evaluation generates telemetry.

Examples include:

- evaluation started
- rule matched
- approval required
- execution denied
- restriction applied
- evaluation completed

Policy decisions shall be auditable.

---

# 16. Policy Governance

Policies themselves are governed.

Governance includes:

- approval workflow
- version control
- ownership
- auditing
- lifecycle management
- compliance review

Governance prevents unauthorized policy changes.

---

# 17. Policy Versioning

Policies evolve through versioning.

```
Policy v1

↓

Policy v2

↓

Policy v3
```

Historical versions remain preserved.

Existing executions continue using the version defined by governance rules.

---

# 18. Design Principles

Policies follow these principles.

### Explicit

Rules are clearly defined.

---

### Deterministic

Equivalent inputs produce identical decisions.

---

### Enforceable

Every Policy can be executed by the platform.

---

### Observable

Evaluations generate complete telemetry.

---

### Governed

Policy evolution follows controlled processes.

---

# 19. Policy Invariants

The following statements shall always remain true.

1. Every Policy has a unique identity.
2. Policy decisions are deterministic.
3. Policies are continuously enforceable.
4. Policy evaluation is observable.
5. Policies remain versioned.
6. Conflicts are explicitly resolved.
7. Policy changes are governed.
8. Platform governance always has ultimate authority.

These invariants apply throughout the cmdOS platform.

---

# 20. Relationship to the Specification

Policy is the canonical governance mechanism of cmdOS.

Future specifications—including Policy Engine, Authorization Framework, Security Architecture, Trust Framework, Resource Manager, Execution Runtime, Agent Framework, and Kernel—shall define behavior according to the canonical Policy model established in this document.

Every governed action within cmdOS shall be evaluated by applicable Policies.

---

# 21. Summary

Policy defines the enforceable rules that govern every aspect of cmdOS.

It determines whether actions are permitted, restricted, denied, or require additional approval.

By separating governance from implementation, cmdOS ensures that intelligence, execution, and infrastructure always operate within explicit, deterministic, and auditable boundaries.

Policy is therefore the canonical governance primitive of the AI Execution Operating System.
