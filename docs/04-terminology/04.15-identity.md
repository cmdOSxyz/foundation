# Identity

Version: 1.0
Status: Canonical
Category: Foundation

---

# 1. Purpose

This document defines the canonical meaning of **Identity** within cmdOS.

Identity is the authoritative representation of an entity that can be authenticated, authorized, governed, and held accountable within the AI Execution Operating System.

Every governed action originates from an Identity.

Identity establishes trust, ownership, accountability, and responsibility across the platform.

---

# 2. Definition

An **Identity** is a uniquely identifiable entity recognized by cmdOS that is capable of participating in governed interactions.

An Identity may represent:

- a human
- an AI Agent
- a service account
- an application
- an organization
- a device

Identity is independent of implementation.

---

# 3. Identity Is Not a User

A User is one type of Identity.

Identity is the broader architectural concept.

```
Identity

├── User

├── Agent

├── Service Account

├── Organization

└── Device
```

Every User is an Identity.

Not every Identity is a User.

---

# 4. Identity Is Not Authentication

Authentication verifies Identity.

Identity exists before authentication occurs.

```
Identity

↓

Authentication

↓

Verified Identity
```

Authentication proves identity.

It does not create identity.

---

# 5. Identity Is Not Authorization

Identity answers:

> "Who is acting?"

Authorization answers:

> "What is this Identity allowed to do?"

Authorization depends upon Identity.

Identity does not depend upon Authorization.

---

# 6. Identity Characteristics

Every Identity shall be:

- unique
- persistent
- verifiable
- governable
- observable
- accountable
- versioned

Identity remains stable throughout its lifecycle.

---

# 7. Identity Identity

Every Identity shall possess a globally unique identifier.

Minimum metadata includes:

- Identity ID
- Identity Type
- Display Name
- Status
- Owner
- Organization
- Creation Timestamp
- Version

Identity identifiers shall never be reused.

---

# 8. Identity Lifecycle

Every Identity progresses through a managed lifecycle.

```
Registered

↓

Verified

↓

Active

↓

Suspended

↓

Reactivated

↓

Retired
```

Alternative terminal states include:

- Revoked
- Deleted
- Expired

Lifecycle transitions shall remain observable.

---

# 9. Identity Ownership

Every Identity has a responsible owner.

Ownership may belong to:

- an individual
- an organization
- the platform
- an automated provisioning system

Ownership determines governance responsibility.

---

# 10. Identity Relationships

Identities may establish governed relationships.

Examples include:

- member of organization
- owner of resource
- manager of agent
- delegate of user
- administrator of workspace

Relationships shall remain explicit.

---

# 11. Identity Trust

Every Identity possesses a Trust level.

Trust may consider:

- verification status
- authentication strength
- historical behavior
- organizational membership
- reputation
- compliance

Trust influences governance.

Trust never replaces Policy.

---

# 12. Identity Credentials

Identity may possess one or more credentials.

Examples include:

- password
- passkey
- cryptographic key
- wallet address
- API key
- certificate
- biometric factor

Credentials authenticate Identity.

They are not the Identity itself.

---

# 13. Identity Delegation

Authority may be delegated between Identities.

Delegation shall define:

- delegator
- delegate
- scope
- duration
- restrictions

Delegation shall remain governed and observable.

---

# 14. Identity Observability

Every significant Identity action generates telemetry.

Examples include:

- registered
- verified
- authenticated
- suspended
- delegated
- reactivated
- retired

Identity history shall remain permanently observable.

---

# 15. Identity Governance

Identity remains governed throughout its lifecycle.

Governance includes:

- registration
- verification
- lifecycle management
- ownership validation
- policy enforcement
- auditing

Governance establishes accountability.

---

# 16. Identity Versioning

Identity metadata may evolve.

```
Identity v1

↓

Identity v2

↓

Identity v3
```

Identity ID remains unchanged.

Historical versions remain preserved.

---

# 17. Identity Classification

cmdOS recognizes multiple Identity classes.

### Human Identity

Represents individual users.

---

### Agent Identity

Represents autonomous execution entities.

---

### Service Identity

Represents software services and APIs.

---

### Organization Identity

Represents legal or logical organizations.

---

### Device Identity

Represents governed hardware devices.

Additional Identity classes may be introduced without changing the architectural model.

---

# 18. Design Principles

Identity follows these principles.

### Unique

Every Identity is globally unique.

---

### Persistent

Identity survives across Sessions and Executions.

---

### Verifiable

Identity can be authenticated.

---

### Governed

Identity remains subject to platform governance.

---

### Accountable

Every governed action can be traced back to an Identity.

---

# 19. Identity Invariants

The following statements shall always remain true.

1. Every Identity has a unique identifier.
2. Identity exists independently of authentication.
3. Authentication verifies Identity.
4. Authorization depends upon Identity.
5. Identity remains governed throughout its lifecycle.
6. Identity actions are observable.
7. Identity ownership is explicit.
8. Every governed action is attributable to an Identity.

These invariants apply throughout the cmdOS platform.

---

# 20. Relationship to the Specification

Identity is the canonical trust anchor of cmdOS.

Future specifications—including Identity Framework, Authentication System, Authorization Framework, Trust Framework, Organization Framework, Session Manager, Agent Framework, Security Architecture, and Kernel—shall define behavior according to the canonical Identity model established in this document.

Every governed interaction within cmdOS shall originate from a verified Identity.

---

# 21. Summary

Identity is the authoritative representation of an entity within cmdOS.

It establishes uniqueness, accountability, governance, and trust across users, agents, services, organizations, and devices.

By separating Identity from authentication, authorization, and execution, cmdOS provides a consistent foundation for secure, scalable, and auditable operation throughout the AI Execution Operating System.

Identity is therefore the canonical trust primitive of cmdOS.
