# Goal

Version: 1.0
Status: Canonical
Category: Foundation

---

# 1. Purpose

This document defines the canonical meaning of **Goal** within cmdOS.

A Goal is the canonical representation of a desired outcome that guides intelligent behavior within the platform.

Goals provide direction for Reasoning, Planning, Decision, and Execution.

Every intentional activity within cmdOS shall originate from one or more Goals.

---

# 2. Definition

A **Goal** is a governed representation of a desired future state that the platform seeks to achieve.

A Goal defines:

- objective
- expected outcome
- success criteria
- constraints
- priority
- ownership

Goals describe desired results.

They do not prescribe implementation.

---

# 3. Goal Is Not an Intent

Intent expresses desire.

Goal formalizes that desire into an actionable objective.

```
Intent

↓

Goal

↓

Reasoning
```

One Intent may produce multiple Goals.

---

# 4. Goal Is Not a Task

A Goal defines *what* should be achieved.

A Task defines *work* performed to achieve it.

```
Goal

↓

Task A

Task B

Task C
```

Tasks exist in support of Goals.

---

# 5. Goal Is Not an Execution

Execution performs work.

Goals define desired outcomes.

```
Goal

↓

Planning

↓

Execution
```

Execution may complete without fully achieving the Goal.

---

# 6. Goal Characteristics

Every Goal shall be:

- uniquely identifiable
- measurable
- governed
- observable
- prioritizable
- traceable
- evaluable

Goals shall define outcomes rather than implementation details.

---

# 7. Goal Identity

Every Goal shall possess a globally unique identifier.

Minimum metadata includes:

- Goal ID
- Name
- Description
- Owner
- Status
- Priority
- Creation Timestamp
- Version

Identity enables governance and lifecycle management.

---

# 8. Goal Lifecycle

Every Goal progresses through a managed lifecycle.

```
Defined

↓

Approved

↓

Active

↓

Achieved

↓

Archived
```

Alternative terminal states include:

- Cancelled
- Failed
- Superseded
- Expired

Lifecycle transitions shall remain observable.

---

# 9. Goal Inputs

Goals may originate from:

- User Intent
- Organizations
- Policies
- Workflows
- Agents
- External Events
- Scheduled Processes

Goal creation shall remain governed.

---

# 10. Goal Outputs

Goals may produce:

- Planning requests
- Reasoning objectives
- Execution requests
- Monitoring requirements
- Evaluation criteria
- Notifications

Goals initiate intelligent platform behavior.

---

# 11. Goal Hierarchy

Goals may be hierarchical.

```
Strategic Goal

├── Operational Goal

│   ├── Task Goal

│   └── Task Goal

└── Validation Goal
```

Child Goals inherit governance from parent Goals unless explicitly overridden.

---

# 12. Goal Priority

Goals may define execution priority.

Priority considerations include:

- urgency
- importance
- deadlines
- dependencies
- organizational policy
- resource availability

Priority influences scheduling.

Priority does not override governance.

---

# 13. Goal Constraints

Goals may define constraints.

Examples include:

- budget
- deadlines
- compliance
- permissions
- execution limits
- geographic restrictions
- resource limits

Constraints remain explicit.

---

# 14. Goal Success Criteria

Every measurable Goal shall define success criteria.

Examples include:

- completion status
- quality thresholds
- time limits
- accuracy
- cost limits
- user satisfaction
- policy compliance

Success evaluation shall remain objective whenever possible.

---

# 15. Goal Observability

Every significant Goal event generates telemetry.

Examples include:

- created
- approved
- activated
- paused
- resumed
- achieved
- failed
- archived

Goal history shall remain permanently observable.

---

# 16. Goal Governance

Goals remain governed throughout their lifecycle.

Governance includes:

- ownership
- approval
- policy validation
- compliance
- auditing
- accountability

Governance establishes trustworthy objective management.

---

# 17. Goal Evaluation

Goal progress may be continuously evaluated.

Evaluation may consider:

- completion percentage
- milestone achievement
- resource consumption
- execution quality
- policy compliance
- overall effectiveness

Evaluation remains implementation independent.

---

# 18. Design Principles

Goals follow these principles.

### Outcome-Oriented

Goals define desired outcomes.

---

### Measurable

Goals support objective evaluation.

---

### Governed

Goals follow platform governance.

---

### Hierarchical

Goals may compose larger objectives.

---

### Observable

Goal lifecycle activities generate telemetry.

---

# 19. Goal Invariants

The following statements shall always remain true.

1. Every Goal has a unique identity.
2. Every Goal defines a desired outcome.
3. Every Goal has an explicit lifecycle state.
4. Goals remain governed.
5. Goal progress remains observable.
6. Goals may produce Planning objectives.
7. Goal evaluation remains traceable.
8. Goals never bypass platform governance.

These invariants apply throughout the cmdOS platform.

---

# 20. Relationship to the Specification

Goal is the canonical objective primitive within cmdOS.

Future specifications—including Goal Management, Objective Tracking, Planning Engine, Autonomous Agents, Multi-Agent Collaboration, Workflow Engine, Performance Evaluation, Success Metrics, and Kernel—shall define behavior according to the canonical Goal model established in this document.

Every intentional activity within cmdOS shall originate from one or more Goals.

---

# 21. Summary

A Goal is the governed representation of a desired outcome within cmdOS.

It transforms Intent into explicit objectives that guide Reasoning, Planning, Decision, and Execution while preserving governance, measurability, observability, and accountability.

By separating Goals from Intents, Tasks, and Executions, cmdOS establishes a scalable objective-management architecture capable of supporting autonomous agents, enterprise governance, continuous evaluation, and long-term intelligent execution.

Goal is therefore the canonical objective primitive of the AI Execution Operating System.
