# Policy

Version: 1.0  
Status: Canonical  
Category: Foundation

---

# 1. Purpose

This document defines the canonical meaning of **Policy** within cmdOS.

Policy is the canonical governance mechanism that defines rules, restrictions, permissions, requirements, and behavioral boundaries within the AI Execution Operating System.

Policies ensure that every Object, Actor, Agent, Tool, Resource, and Execution operates according to defined principles.

Every governed operation within cmdOS shall be evaluated against one or more Policies.

---

# 2. Definition

A **Policy** is a governed rule definition that determines whether an operation, behavior, transition, or access request is allowed, restricted, modified, or rejected.

A Policy defines:

- conditions
- permissions
- restrictions
- obligations
- approvals
- enforcement behavior

Policies define what is allowed.

They do not perform execution.

---

# 3. Policy Is Not Permission

Permission defines an allowed capability.

Policy defines the rules controlling that permission.

```
Policy

↓

Permission

↓

Authorization Decision
```

Policy determines how permissions are applied.

---

# 4. Policy Is Not a Rule

Rules are individual conditions.

Policies are collections of governed rules.

```
Rule A

+

Rule B

+

Rule C

↓

Policy
```

Policy provides broader governance.

---

# 5. Policy Is Not a Decision

Decision is the result of evaluating a Policy.

```
Policy

↓

Evaluation

↓

Decision
```

Policy defines logic.

Decision applies logic.

---

# 6. Policy Characteristics

Every Policy shall be:

- explicit
- enforceable
- observable
- versioned
- traceable
- governed
- testable

Policies shall remain understandable and auditable.

---

# 7. Policy Identity

Every Policy shall possess a globally unique identifier.

Minimum metadata includes:

- Policy ID
- Name
- Description
- Owner
- Version
- Scope
- Status
- Creation Timestamp

Identity enables governance tracking.

---

- encryption requirements
- access restrictions

---

Controls allowed operations.

Examples:

- who can access resources
- what actions are permitted

---

## Execution Policy

Examples:

- execution limits
- allowed tools
- resource restrictions

---

## Agent Policy

Examples:

- allowed capabilities
- approval requirements
- operating boundaries

---

## Data Policy

Controls information usage.

Examples:

- privacy rules
- retention
- sharing restrictions

---

## Resource Policy

Controls resource consumption.

Examples:

- quotas
- cost limits
- allocation rules

---

## Compliance Policy

Controls regulatory requirements.

Examples:

- audit requirements
- reporting rules
- operational standards

# 10. Policy Scope

Every Policy shall define its scope.

Scope may include:

- Organization
- User
- Agent
- Object
- Resource
- Workflow
- Execution
- Environment

Policies shall only affect authorized scope.

---

# 11. Policy Evaluation

Policy evaluation determines whether an operation is allowed.

Evaluation may consider:

- Identity
- Context
- State
- Intent
- Resource availability
- Risk level
- Previous Events

Evaluation shall remain observable.

---

# 12. Policy Decision

Policy evaluation produces a Decision.

Possible outcomes include:

```
Allow

↓

Deny

↓

Require Approval

↓

Modify

↓

Escalate
```

Every Decision shall preserve Policy references.

---

# 13. Policy Enforcement

Policy enforcement occurs before and during execution.

Enforcement points may include:

- Kernel
- Runtime
- Permission Layer
- Execution Engine
- Tool Layer

Policies cannot be bypassed.

---

# 14. Dynamic Policy

Policies may adapt according to conditions.

Dynamic factors include:

- risk level
- environment
- user behavior
- resource availability
- security status

Dynamic changes shall remain observable.

---

# 15. Policy Versioning

Policies evolve through versions.

```
Policy v1

↓

Policy v2

↓

Policy v3
```

Previous versions remain available for auditing.

---

# 16. Policy Conflicts

Multiple Policies may apply to one operation.

Conflict resolution may consider:

- priority
- authority level
- specificity
- organization rules

Conflicts shall be resolved deterministically.

---

# 17. Policy Priority

Policies may define priority.

Example:

```
System Security Policy

↓

Organization Policy

↓

User Preference
```

Higher authority Policies override lower authority Policies.

---

# 18. Policy Observability

Every significant Policy operation generates telemetry.

Examples:

- created
- evaluated
- approved
- enforced
- violated
- updated

Policy history shall remain observable.

---

# 19. Policy Violation

A Policy violation occurs when an operation violates governance requirements.

Violations may trigger:

- rejection
- warning
- escalation
- audit record
- remediation workflow

Violations shall remain traceable.

---

# 20. Policy Governance

Policies themselves are governed Objects.

Governance includes:

- ownership
- approval
- review
- version control
- auditing
- retirement

Policy changes require controlled management.

---

# 21. Policy Security

Policies protect the system by enforcing:

- least privilege
- zero trust
- controlled execution
- data protection
- accountability

Security decisions shall originate from governed Policies.

---

# 22. Design Principles

Policies follow these principles.

### Explicit

Rules are clearly defined.

---

### Deterministic

Same conditions produce consistent decisions.

---

### Observable

Policy evaluation remains visible.

---

### Adaptable

Policies can evolve safely.

---

### Enforceable

Policies affect actual behavior.

---

# 23. Policy Invariants

The following statements shall always remain true.

1. Every Policy has a unique identity.
2. Every Policy has an owner.
3. Every Policy has defined scope.
4. Every execution is subject to Policy evaluation.
5. Policy decisions are traceable.
6. Policy versions are preserved.
7. Policy violations are observable.
8. Policies cannot be bypassed.

These invariants apply throughout the cmdOS platform.

---

# 24. Relationship to the Specification

Policy is the canonical governance primitive within cmdOS.

Future specifications—including Governance Engine, Authorization System, Permission Framework, Security Architecture, Agent Safety Layer, Compliance Framework, Risk Engine, and Kernel—shall define behavior according to the canonical Policy model established in this document.

Every controlled behavior within cmdOS shall operate according to one or more Policies.

---

# 25. Summary

Policy defines the rules that govern every operation within cmdOS.

It creates the control layer between Intent and Execution by determining what Actors, Agents, Tools, Resources, and Systems are allowed to do.

By separating Policy from Permission, Rule, and Decision, cmdOS establishes a scalable governance architecture capable of supporting autonomous AI systems while maintaining security, compliance, transparency, and human control.

Policy is therefore the canonical governance rule primitive of the AI Execution Operating System.
