# Permission

Version: 1.0
Status: Canonical
Category: Foundation

---

# 1. Purpose

This document defines the canonical meaning of **Permission** within cmdOS.

Permissions define the authority granted to an identity to perform specific actions within the platform.

Permissions establish what an entity is allowed to do.

Policies determine whether those permissions may be exercised under current conditions.

---

# 2. Definition

A **Permission** is an explicit authorization granting an identity the ability to perform one or more defined actions on one or more governed resources.

Permissions represent granted authority.

They do not guarantee execution.

Execution remains subject to Policy evaluation.

---

# 3. Permission Is Not Policy

Permission grants authority.

Policy governs authority.

Example:

```
Permission

↓

May Execute

↓

Policy

↓

Allowed?
```

Permission answers:

> "Can this identity perform this action?"

Policy answers:

> "Should this action be allowed right now?"

---

# 4. Permission Is Not Authentication

Authentication establishes identity.

Permission establishes authority.

```
Authentication

↓

Identity

↓

Permission

↓

Authorized Actions
```

Identity must exist before Permissions can be evaluated.

---

# 5. Permission Is Not Role

Roles are collections of Permissions.

Permission is the smallest unit of authority.

```
Role

↓

Permissions

↓

Actions
```

Roles simplify administration.

Permissions define capability.

---

# 6. Permission Characteristics

Every Permission shall be:

- explicit
- assignable
- revocable
- observable
- versioned
- auditable
- governed

Implicit Permissions are prohibited.

---

# 7. Permission Identity

Every Permission shall possess a unique identifier.

Minimum metadata includes:

- Permission ID
- Name
- Description
- Version
- Owner
- Scope
- Status
- Creation Date

Identity enables governance and auditing.

---

# 8. Permission Scope

Permissions may apply to:

- Objects
- Resources
- Capabilities
- Workflows
- Executions
- Sessions
- Agents
- Organizations
- APIs

Scope shall be explicitly declared.

---

# 9. Permission Lifecycle

Every Permission progresses through a managed lifecycle.

```
Defined

↓

Approved

↓

Assigned

↓

Active

↓

Revoked

↓

Archived
```

Lifecycle transitions shall remain observable.

---

# 10. Permission Assignment

Permissions may be assigned to:

- Users
- Agents
- Roles
- Organizations
- Sessions
- Service Accounts

Assignments shall be explicitly recorded.

---

# 11. Permission Evaluation

Permission evaluation determines whether authority exists.

Evaluation considers:

- identity
- assigned permissions
- inherited permissions
- delegation
- scope
- ownership

Permission evaluation does not consider runtime policy.

---

# 12. Permission Delegation

Permissions may be delegated when permitted by governance.

Delegation shall specify:

- delegator
- delegate
- scope
- duration
- restrictions

Delegated Permissions remain governed.

---

# 13. Permission Revocation

Permissions may be revoked at any time.

Revocation may occur because of:

- expiration
- administrative action
- policy change
- security incident
- organizational change

Revocation takes effect immediately unless governance specifies otherwise.

---

# 14. Permission Inheritance

Permissions may be inherited.

Examples include:

```
Organization

↓

Team

↓

Role

↓

User
```

Inheritance rules shall remain deterministic.

---

# 15. Permission Observability

Every Permission operation generates telemetry.

Examples include:

- assigned
- delegated
- revoked
- expired
- evaluated
- denied

Permission history shall remain permanently observable.

---

# 16. Permission Governance

Permissions remain governed throughout their lifecycle.

Governance includes:

- approval
- assignment validation
- delegation rules
- revocation rules
- auditing
- compliance review

Governance prevents privilege escalation.

---

# 17. Permission Models

cmdOS supports multiple authorization models.

Examples include:

### Role-Based Access Control (RBAC)

Permissions assigned through Roles.

---

### Attribute-Based Access Control (ABAC)

Permissions evaluated using attributes.

---

### Capability-Based Access Control

Authority granted through executable Capabilities.

---

### Relationship-Based Access Control

Authority derived from defined relationships.

Future authorization models may be introduced without changing the canonical Permission model.

---

# 18. Design Principles

Permissions follow these principles.

### Explicit

Authority is explicitly granted.

---

### Least Privilege

Only necessary Permissions shall be assigned.

---

### Revocable

Authority can be withdrawn.

---

### Observable

Permission changes generate telemetry.

---

### Governed

Assignments follow platform governance.

---

# 19. Permission Invariants

The following statements shall always remain true.

1. Every Permission has a unique identity.
2. Permissions grant authority, not execution.
3. Permission assignments are explicit.
4. Permissions remain governed.
5. Permission evaluations are observable.
6. Delegation follows governance rules.
7. Revocation is supported throughout the lifecycle.
8. Policies always retain final authority over execution.

These invariants apply throughout the cmdOS platform.

---

# 20. Relationship to the Specification

Permission is the canonical authorization primitive within cmdOS.

Future specifications—including Authorization Framework, Identity Framework, Security Architecture, RBAC, ABAC, Capability Access Control, Trust Framework, Policy Engine, and Kernel—shall define behavior according to the canonical Permission model established in this document.

Every privileged action within cmdOS shall require one or more Permissions.

---

# 21. Summary

Permission defines the authority granted to perform specific actions within cmdOS.

It establishes who may access resources, execute capabilities, manage workflows, or operate platform components while remaining continuously governed by platform Policies.

By separating authority from governance, cmdOS enables secure, scalable, and auditable authorization across the AI Execution Operating System.

Permission is therefore the canonical authorization primitive of cmdOS.
