# Role

Version: 1.0
Status: Canonical
Category: Foundation

---

# 1. Purpose

This document defines the canonical meaning of **Role** within cmdOS.

A Role groups related Permissions into a reusable authorization profile.

Roles simplify authorization management while preserving the principle of least privilege.

They provide a scalable mechanism for assigning authority across users, agents, organizations, and services.

---

# 2. Definition

A **Role** is a named collection of Permissions representing a defined set of responsibilities within the platform.

Roles simplify Permission management.

They do not replace Permissions.

Every Role exists solely as an authorization abstraction.

---

# 3. Role Is Not Permission

Permissions are individual units of authority.

Roles organize Permissions.

```
Role

↓

Permissions

↓

Authorized Actions
```

Permissions define capability.

Roles define responsibility.

---

# 4. Role Is Not Identity

Identity answers:

> "Who is performing this action?"

Role answers:

> "What responsibilities has this identity been assigned?"

One Identity may possess multiple Roles.

One Role may belong to many Identities.

---

# 5. Role Is Not Policy

Roles grant collections of Permissions.

Policies determine whether those Permissions may be exercised.

```
Identity

↓

Role

↓

Permissions

↓

Policy

↓

Execution
```

Roles provide authority.

Policies govern authority.

---

# 6. Role Characteristics

Every Role shall be:

- uniquely identifiable
- reusable
- assignable
- revocable
- observable
- versioned
- governed

Roles shall never contain executable logic.

---

# 7. Role Identity

Every Role shall possess a globally unique identifier.

Minimum metadata includes:

- Role ID
- Name
- Description
- Version
- Owner
- Status
- Creation Date
- Organization Scope

Identity supports governance and auditing.

---

# 8. Role Lifecycle

Every Role progresses through a managed lifecycle.

```
Defined

↓

Validated

↓

Approved

↓

Active

↓

Deprecated

↓

Retired
```

Lifecycle transitions shall remain observable.

---

# 9. Role Composition

Roles consist of one or more Permissions.

Example:

```
Administrator

↓

Manage Users

Manage Policies

Manage Resources

Manage Organizations
```

Roles may evolve through versioning.

---

# 10. Role Assignment

Roles may be assigned to:

- Users
- Agents
- Service Accounts
- Organizations
- Groups

Assignments shall be explicitly recorded.

---

# 11. Multiple Roles

An identity may possess multiple Roles simultaneously.

Example:

```
User

↓

Developer

Security Reviewer

AI Operator
```

Permission aggregation shall follow deterministic rules.

---

# 12. Role Hierarchy

Roles may inherit other Roles.

Example:

```
Administrator

↓

Manager

↓

Operator

↓

Viewer
```

Inheritance shall remain explicit.

Circular inheritance is prohibited.

---

# 13. Role Scope

Roles may be scoped to:

- Platform
- Organization
- Project
- Workspace
- Session
- Resource Group

Scope shall be explicitly declared.

---

# 14. Role Evaluation

Role evaluation determines which Permissions are available to an identity.

Evaluation considers:

- assigned Roles
- inherited Roles
- delegation
- organizational scope
- activation status

Role evaluation does not replace Policy evaluation.

---

# 15. Role Observability

Every Role operation generates telemetry.

Examples include:

- role created
- role assigned
- role revoked
- permission added
- permission removed
- inheritance updated

Role history shall remain permanently observable.

---

# 16. Role Governance

Roles remain governed throughout their lifecycle.

Governance includes:

- approval
- ownership
- version control
- auditing
- delegation rules
- compliance review

Governance prevents unauthorized privilege escalation.

---

# 17. Role Versioning

Roles evolve through versioning.

```
Role v1

↓

Role v2

↓

Role v3
```

Historical versions remain available for auditing and rollback.

Assignments reference governed Role versions.

---

# 18. Design Principles

Roles follow these principles.

### Reusable

Roles eliminate repetitive Permission assignments.

---

### Explicit

Role membership is clearly defined.

---

### Governed

Role evolution follows platform governance.

---

### Observable

Every Role change produces telemetry.

---

### Least Privilege

Roles should grant only the Permissions required to perform assigned responsibilities.

---

# 19. Role Invariants

The following statements shall always remain true.

1. Every Role has a unique identity.
2. Roles contain Permissions.
3. Roles never replace Policies.
4. Role assignments are explicit.
5. Role evaluation is observable.
6. Role inheritance is deterministic.
7. Roles remain governed.
8. Least privilege remains the default design principle.

These invariants apply throughout the cmdOS platform.

---

# 20. Relationship to the Specification

Role is the canonical authorization grouping mechanism within cmdOS.

Future specifications—including RBAC, Identity Framework, Organization Framework, Administration System, Authorization Framework, Policy Engine, Trust Framework, and Kernel—shall define behavior according to the canonical Role model established in this document.

Every scalable authorization model within cmdOS shall build upon Roles and Permissions.

---

# 21. Summary

A Role is a reusable collection of Permissions representing a defined set of responsibilities within cmdOS.

Roles simplify authorization management while preserving explicit governance, deterministic evaluation, and least-privilege access control.

By separating responsibilities from individual Permissions, cmdOS enables scalable, maintainable, and auditable authorization across the AI Execution Operating System.

Role is therefore the canonical authorization grouping primitive of cmdOS.
