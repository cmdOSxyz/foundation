# Decision

Version: 1.0  
Status: Canonical  
Category: Foundation

---

# 1. Purpose

This document defines the canonical meaning of **Decision** within cmdOS.

Decision is the canonical representation of a selected outcome produced after evaluating Intent, Context, Knowledge, Policies, Constraints, and available options.

Decisions transform understanding into direction.

Every autonomous or human-guided execution within cmdOS shall be based on one or more Decisions.

---

# 2. Definition

A **Decision** is a governed selection of an intended course of action among one or more possible alternatives.

A Decision defines:

- selected option
- reasoning basis
- supporting evidence
- constraints considered
- confidence level
- authority source

Decision represents what should be done.

It does not perform execution.

---

# 3. Decision Is Not Intent

Intent describes desired outcomes.

Decision selects the approach to achieve them.

```
Intent

↓

Reasoning

↓

Decision
```

Intent defines the desire.

Decision defines the chosen direction.

---

# 4. Decision Is Not Reasoning

Reasoning produces analysis and evaluation.

Decision produces a selected outcome.

```
Reasoning

↓

Decision

↓

Planning
```

Reasoning explains possibilities.

Decision chooses.

---

# 5. Decision Is Not an Action

Action performs operations.

Decision determines which operations should occur.

```
Decision

↓

Action

↓

Execution
```

A Decision may generate multiple Actions.

---

# 6. Decision Characteristics

Every Decision shall be:

- identifiable
- explainable
- governed
- traceable
- confidence-aware
- reviewable
- accountable

Decisions shall preserve the basis behind selection.

---

# 7. Decision Identity

Every Decision shall possess a globally unique identifier.

Minimum metadata includes:

- Decision ID
- Source Actor
- Related Intent
- Context Reference
- Timestamp
- Status
- Confidence Score
- Version

Identity enables accountability.

---

# 8. Decision Lifecycle

Every Decision progresses through a managed lifecycle.

```
Created

↓

Analyzed

↓

Evaluated

↓

Approved

↓

Applied

↓

Reviewed

↓

Archived
```

Alternative states include:

- Rejected
- Cancelled
- Superseded
- Expired

Lifecycle transitions shall remain observable.

---

# 9. Decision Sources

Decisions may originate from:

- Human Actors
- AI Agents
- Policies
- Workflows
- Planning Systems
- External Systems

Every Decision shall identify its source.

---

# 10. Decision Types

cmdOS recognizes multiple Decision categories.

---

## Execution Decision

Determines operational actions.

Examples:

- which Tool to use
- when to execute
- how to complete a Task

---

## Planning Decision

Determines execution strategy.

Examples:

- task ordering
- resource allocation
- workflow selection

---

## Security Decision

Determines access and protection.

Examples:

- allow access
- require approval
- block operation

---

## Resource Decision

Determines resource usage.

Examples:

- model selection
- infrastructure allocation
- cost optimization

---

## Agent Decision

Represents autonomous Agent choices.

Examples:

- selecting next action
- adapting strategy
- requesting assistance

---

# 11. Decision Inputs

A Decision may consider:

- Intent
- Context
- Knowledge
- Memory
- State
- Policies
- Resources
- Constraints
- Previous Results

Decision inputs shall remain traceable.

---

# 12. Decision Output

A Decision may produce:

- selected action
- selected workflow
- selected Tool
- execution strategy
- approval request
- escalation request

Outputs shall preserve decision history.

---

# 13. Decision Reasoning

Every Decision may include reasoning information.

Reasoning may contain:

- evaluated options
- assumptions
- evidence
- constraints
- confidence
- trade-offs

Reasoning improves transparency.

---

# 14. Decision Confidence

Decisions may include confidence levels.

Confidence may consider:

- available information
- uncertainty
- historical success
- policy requirements
- risk factors

Low-confidence Decisions may require review.

---

# 15. Decision Authority

Every Decision shall operate under authority boundaries.

Authority may come from:

- User permission
- Agent permission
- Organization policy
- System policy

A Decision cannot exceed its authority.

---

# 16. Human Approval

Certain Decisions may require Human approval.

Approval requirements may depend on:

- risk level
- financial impact
- security sensitivity
- policy rules

Human approval remains part of governance.

---

# 17. Decision Conflicts

Multiple Decisions may conflict.

Conflict resolution may consider:

- authority
- priority
- confidence
- policy constraints
- timestamps

Resolution shall remain deterministic.

---

# 18. Decision Observability

Every significant Decision event generates telemetry.

Examples:

- created
- evaluated
- approved
- rejected
- executed
- overridden

Decision history shall remain permanently observable.

---

# 19. Decision Governance

Decisions remain governed throughout their lifecycle.

Governance includes:

- authority validation
- policy evaluation
- audit logging
- explanation requirements
- approval controls

Governance ensures responsible autonomy.

---

# 20. Autonomous Decision Making

Agents may produce autonomous Decisions.

Autonomous Decisions shall operate within:

- Identity boundaries
- Permission limits
- Policy constraints
- Risk thresholds

Autonomy does not remove accountability.

---

# 21. Decision Learning

Decision history may improve future decisions.

Learning may use:

- previous outcomes
- success rates
- user feedback
- execution Results

Learning shall preserve governance.

---

# 22. Design Principles

Decisions follow these principles.

### Explainable

The basis of a Decision can be understood.

---

### Governed

Decisions follow authority and policy.

---

### Adaptive

Decisions improve through experience.

---

### Accountable

Every Decision has a responsible source.

---

### Observable

Decision history remains visible.

---

# 23. Decision Invariants

The following statements shall always remain true.

1. Every Decision has a unique identity.
2. Every Decision has a source.
3. Every Decision operates within authority.
4. Every Decision is traceable.
5. Every Decision may be explained.
6. Every Decision follows Policies.
7. Decision history is preserved.
8. Decisions cannot directly bypass execution governance.

These invariants apply throughout the cmdOS platform.

---

# 24. Relationship to the Specification

Decision is the canonical choice primitive within cmdOS.

Future specifications—including Decision Engine, Reasoning Engine, Planning System, Agent Autonomy Framework, Risk Engine, Human Approval System, and Kernel Governance Layer—shall define behavior according to the canonical Decision model established in this document.

Every meaningful choice within cmdOS shall be represented as one or more Decisions.

---

# 25. Summary

Decision is the bridge between intelligence and execution within cmdOS.

It transforms analysis, knowledge, policies, and context into controlled choices while preserving transparency, authority, and accountability.

By separating Decisions from Intent, Reasoning, Actions, and Executions, cmdOS establishes a foundation for trustworthy autonomous AI systems capable of making decisions while remaining secure and governed.

Decision is therefore the canonical choice primitive of the AI Execution Operating System.
