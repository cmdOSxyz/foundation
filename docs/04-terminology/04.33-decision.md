# Decision

Version: 1.0
Status: Canonical
Category: Foundation

---

# 1. Purpose

This document defines the canonical meaning of **Decision** within cmdOS.

A Decision is the canonical representation of an approved conclusion produced by Reasoning or Planning.

Decisions authorize, modify, delay, reject, or terminate future execution.

Every governed action within cmdOS shall be traceable to one or more Decisions.

---

# 2. Definition

A **Decision** is a governed, traceable, and authoritative determination that resolves one or more alternatives in pursuit of an objective.

A Decision may originate from:

- Reasoning
- Planning
- Policy Evaluation
- Human Approval
- Agent Deliberation
- Automated Rules

A Decision authorizes action.

It does not perform action.

---

# 3. Decision Is Not Reasoning

Reasoning evaluates information.

Decision records the selected outcome.

```
Reasoning

↓

Decision

↓

Execution
```

Reasoning produces understanding.

Decision produces authorization.

---

# 4. Decision Is Not Planning

Planning organizes execution.

Decision approves or modifies the plan.

```
Planning

↓

Decision

↓

Execution
```

Planning proposes.

Decision commits.

---

# 5. Decision Is Not Execution

Execution performs work.

Decision authorizes work.

```
Decision

↓

Execution
```

Execution shall not begin unless authorized according to applicable governance policies.

---

# 6. Decision Characteristics

Every Decision shall be:

- uniquely identifiable
- governed
- authoritative
- traceable
- observable
- explainable
- immutable after publication

Decisions establish execution authority.

---

# 7. Decision Identity

Every Decision shall possess a globally unique identifier.

Minimum metadata includes:

- Decision ID
- Objective
- Decision Type
- Status
- Decision Maker
- Confidence
- Timestamp
- Version

Identity enables governance and auditing.

---

# 8. Decision Lifecycle

Every Decision progresses through a managed lifecycle.

```
Proposed

↓

Evaluated

↓

Approved

↓

Published

↓

Referenced

↓

Archived
```

Alternative terminal states include:

- Rejected
- Cancelled
- Superseded

Published Decisions remain immutable.

---

A Decision may consider:

- Reasoning
- Planning

- Constraints
- Knowledge

- External Information

Inputs shall remain traceable.

# 10. Decision Outputs

A Decision may produce:

- execution authorization
- execution denial
- plan approval
- resource allocation
- policy exception
- escalation
- recommendation
- termination request

Outputs may initiate subsequent platform processes.

# 12. Decision Authority

Every Decision shall identify its authority.

Authority may originate from:

- Users
- Organizations
- Policies
- Agents
- Approval Committees
- Automated Governance Systems

Authority shall remain explicit.

---

# 13. Decision Confidence

A Decision may include confidence information.

Confidence indicators may consider:

- evidence quality
- reasoning consistency
- model agreement
- policy certainty
- historical accuracy

Confidence informs governance.

Confidence does not replace authorization.

---

# 14. Decision Observability

Every significant Decision event generates telemetry.

Examples include:

- proposed
- evaluated
- approved
- rejected
- published
- referenced
- superseded

Decision history shall remain permanently observable.

---

# 15. Decision Governance

Decisions remain governed throughout their lifecycle.

Governance includes:

- policy validation
- approval requirements
- auditing
- compliance
- explainability
- accountability

Governance establishes trustworthy authorization.

---

# 16. Decision Explainability

Every Decision shall support explanation.

Explanations may include:

- supporting evidence
- evaluated alternatives
- applied policies
- identified constraints
- confidence assessment
- decision rationale

Explainability shall remain implementation independent.

---

# 17. Decision Versioning

Published Decisions are immutable.

Updated conclusions shall create new Decision versions.

```
Decision v1

↓

Decision v2

↓

Decision v3
```

Historical Decisions remain permanently accessible.

---

# 18. Design Principles

Decisions follow these principles.

### Authoritative

Decisions authorize future behavior.

---

### Explainable

Every Decision supports understandable justification.

---

### Governed

Decisions follow platform governance.

---

### Immutable

Published Decisions never change.

---

### Traceable

Every Decision preserves supporting evidence.

---

# 19. Decision Invariants

The following statements shall always remain true.

1. Every Decision has a unique identity.
2. Every Decision has an explicit authority.
3. Every published Decision is immutable.
4. Every Decision is traceable.
5. Every Decision is governed.
6. Every Decision supports explanation.
7. Decision history remains observable.
8. Decisions never bypass platform governance.

These invariants apply throughout the cmdOS platform.

---

# 20. Relationship to the Specification

Decision is the canonical authorization primitive within cmdOS.

Future specifications—including Decision Engine, Policy Engine, Approval Framework, Human-in-the-Loop, Autonomous Agent Governance, Risk Assessment, Execution Authorization, Compliance Framework, and Kernel—shall define behavior according to the canonical Decision model established in this document.

Every governed authorization within cmdOS shall be represented as one or more Decisions.

---

# 21. Summary

A Decision is the governed, authoritative, and traceable conclusion that authorizes future behavior within cmdOS.

It transforms reasoning and planning into explicit commitments while preserving governance, explainability, accountability, and auditability.

By separating Decisions from Reasoning, Planning, and Execution, cmdOS establishes a trustworthy authorization architecture capable of supporting autonomous agents, human oversight, policy enforcement, and enterprise-scale governance.

Decision is therefore the canonical authorization primitive of the AI Execution Operating System.
